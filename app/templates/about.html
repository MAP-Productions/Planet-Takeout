In virtually every neighborhood of every city and town in America, there’s a Chinese takeout. You know the places I’m talking about: the tiny holes-in-the-wall with two or three cafeteria-style tables inside, an open kitchen roaring and rattling with woks and fire, a brisk but friendly counterwoman, and garishly colored photographs of Chinese food advertising the wares.

The takeouts are ubiquitous and seemingly identical. But each is a lens into both the local neighborhood and global immigration patterns. Each sits at the intersection where an immigrant worker meets a person who lives down the street, where the needs of the neighborhood are met by the adaptability of takeout owners, where food is exchanged, money, and perhaps words. Places where communication may be difficult but a sense of community can take root.

Enter Planet Takeout, a participatory documentary project about the Chinese takeout as a vital cultural crossroads.

We’ve spent months documenting four takeouts in different Boston neighborhoods. We spent time in a takeout in Dorchester that created a dollar menu to cater to local teens. We met a second-generation takeout owner who coaches a high school football team in Roxbury. We met a tattoo artist in Jamaica Plain who has constructed an elaborate mythology about the workers at his local takeout, which he freely admits to ordering from three times in the same day.

This is where we need your help. There are 10,000 takeouts in the U.S., which prepare more than 2 million meals every day, and who knows how many around the world.

I bet you’ve gone to one of these takeouts, eaten a few of those meals. Maybe you’re even a regular at one. So much so that the staff knows your order when they hear your voice, perhaps?

Or maybe you or your family has experience working in or running a takeout. You’ve gotten to know your regular customers’ quirks and adjusted to the life of a small business owner far from home.

If we could clone 10,000 versions of ourselves, we could go to your takeout in person – but you’re the one with the story, the one with the relationship across the counter, the one who might know the local characters and the ways the takeout fits into your neighborhood. The one with the memories to share.

So please help us out!

We’ve started with four takeouts in Boston, but we need your energy and creativity to expand outwards and to paint a portrait of the rest of the city, the country and the planet, takeout by takeout.

Val Wang, Producer

Val Wang is a writer and multimedia documentarian who is based in Boston but whose heart is still partly in China. Before Planet Takeout, she produced OpenCourt, WBUR’s cutting-edge project to open the court system through digital technology. She has produced documentaries airing on PBS, National Geographic Channel, and The History Channel and worked for Reuters Television, NBC News, and UNICEF in both New York and Beijing. Her memoir of living in China, Beijing Bastard (working title), is forthcoming from Penguin. She is always thinking about her next meal, and usually the one after that too.

Kelly Creedon is a documentary photographer and multimedia producer based in Boston. Most recently, she is the creator and producer of We Shall Not Be Moved, a multimedia documentary project that tells the story of the growing grassroots anti-foreclosure movement in Massachusetts. Kelly’s work has been featured by the Boston Globe, Boston Magazine, Shelterforce, WBUR, WGBH, and the Bay State Banner, among others. She is a graduate of the Salt Institute for Documentary Studies in Portland, Maine.
